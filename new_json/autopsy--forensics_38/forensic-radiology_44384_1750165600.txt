Forensic radiology. PathologyOutlines.com website. https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicsradiology.html.  Accessed June 17th, 2025.</div><div class="block_content"><div class="block_section" id="definitiongeneral467919"><div class="topicheading_title">Definition / general</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Noninvasive or minimally invasive techniques used to supplement postmortem examination
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="essentialfeatures467920"><div class="topicheading_title">Essential features</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Postmortem radiography is an effective and efficient supplement to autopsy
<li> Postmortem Xray is the most prevalent modality used in the United States
<li> Computed tomography has multiple utilities in postmortem examination
<li> Identification of remains can be accomplished through multiple imaging means
<li> Postmortem radiology is a powerful tool that complements full postmortem examinations but cannot replace them
</ul>

</div></div><div class="block_section" id="terminology467921"><div class="topicheading_title">Terminology</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Forensic / postmortem imaging
<li> Forensic / postmortem radiography
<li> Virtopsy
</ul>
</div></div><div class="block_section" id="historyandgeneralinformation467924"><div class="topicheading_title">History and general information</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Wilhelm Conrad R&#246;ntgen discovered the Xray in 1895
<li> First use in forensic medicine was in the United Kingdom in 1896 (<a href="6369961" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 1984;5:53</a>)
<li> By the early 1900s, Xray techniques for forensic examination became widely adopted throughout the world
<li> First reported application of postmortem computed tomography was in 1977 (<a href="602451" class="bl">Z Rechtsmed 1977;80:227</a>)
<li> Advanced imaging techniques have been adopted more slowly but are becoming more commonplace throughout the world
<ul>
<li> Imaging has been suggested as a potential replacement to autopsy (<a href="22112684" class="bl">Lancet 2012;379:136</a>)
</ul>
<li> Postmortem imaging is often technically superior to antemortem imaging due to lack of motion artifacts and the ability to increase the time of exposure and radiation dose
<li> When performing postmortem imaging, try to remove as much foreign material from the deceased and imaging area as possible to prevent overlay and artifacts
<li> Generally interpreted by pathologists; radiologists may be retained as consultants by some institutions 
<ul>
<li> Specific training programs in forensic radiology have become available through the years (<a href="18929036" class="bl">Clin Radiol 2008;63:1189</a>)
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="xray467925"><div class="topicheading_title">Xray</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Also known as radiograph or roentgenogram
<li> 2 dimensional image created by electromagnetic radiation (Xrays) interacting with structures of various densities between a radiation source and detector
<ul>
<li> Projectional technique: objects closer to the detector appear smaller than same sized objects farther from the detector; unreliable at determining the size of findings
<li> Requires at least 2 views for localization
</ul>
<li> Most utilized modality in the United States
<ul>
<li> Widely available, low cost and most familiar for most pathologists
<li> Requires less technical skill to capture and interpret than other modalities
</ul>
<li> Most useful for differentiating high contrast structures (bone, metallic foreign bodies, etc.) (<a href="7042497" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 1982;19:263</a>)
<li> May also be useful in detecting large fluid or air collections (pneumothorax, pneumoperitoneum, etc.)
<li> Poor at detecting soft tissue changes
<li> Full postmortem radiographic examinations (skeletal survey) are standard of care in pediatric autopsies to exclude occult injuries or natural disease processes
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="computedtomographyct467926"><div class="topicheading_title">Computed tomography (CT)</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> 3 dimensional image created by multiple electromagnetic radiation sources interacting with structures of various densities and multiple detectors (<a href="31005405" class="bl">Acad Radiol 2019;26:820</a>)
<ul>
<li> Allows for multiple planes of view utilizing a single scan
<li> Can accurately determine the size of findings or relationships between landmarks
<li> Adjusting the window (scope of densities viewed) can allow for better differentiation of specific types of tissues (i.e., bone, lung, brain, soft tissue, etc.)
<li> Can also allow for 3 dimensional reconstruction
</ul>
<li> Metallic objects can interfere with scanning (metallic streak artifact)
<li> More widely used in Europe, Australia and Asia than the United States
<ul>
<li> Since it is noninvasive, it may be more acceptable to religious / cultural groups that object to postmortem examinations
</ul>
<li> Better than Xray at detecting most traumatic injuries and air or fluid collections (<a href="25593587" class="bl">J Geriatr Cardiol 2014;11:363</a>,  <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jofri.2014.12.007" class="bl">J Forens Radiol Imaging 2015;3:91</a>)
<li> Useful in asphyxia and gunshot wound related deaths (<a href="25059990" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2014;16:364</a>, <a href="25001050" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2014;16:357</a>)
<ul>
<li> Detailed visualization of the larynx and hyoid bone
<li> Bullets / fragments location; bone fragments directed toward the bullet direction
</ul>
<li> Poor at detecting soft tissue and intra-abdominal pathologies / injuries
<li> Virtopsy has been suggested as an alternative to postmortem examination (<a href="17275386" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2007;9:100</a>)
<ul>
<li> Complete external and internal imaging of the body with contrast
<li> Limited sampling can be utilized for histologic or microbiologic specimens
<li> Significant limitations when compared with traditional autopsy
<li> Most people agree that a hybrid approach with postmortem CT and traditional postmortem examination is superior to either technique alone (<a href="36752321" class="bl">J Forensic Sci 2023;68:524</a>)
</ul>
<li> Significant postmortem artifacts may occur, especially with decompositional changes or heavily refrigerated individuals
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="magneticresonanceimagingmri467927"><div class="topicheading_title">Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> 3 dimensional image created by the interaction of magnetic and ultrasonic waves with various tissues and organs through a series of emitters and detectors
<li> Different phases can be utilized to detect different organs and structures (<a href="24191122" class="bl">Br J Radiol 2014;87:20130567</a>)
<ul>
<li> More detailed images than any of the other modalities and smaller scale resolution
<li> Superior to other modalities for detecting soft tissue findings
</ul>
<li> Not widely available in most areas and largely used as a research modality
<li> Takes longer than other modalities, which limits use
<ul>
<li> Time to image an individual body segment is often greater than the time it would take to perform a full postmortem examination
</ul>
<li> Metallic objects (projectiles or surgical implants) may be at risk of moving due to high power magnetic fields
<li> Significant postmortem artifacts may occur with even mild decompositional changes or any degree of postmortem refrigeration
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="ultrasonography467928"><div class="topicheading_title">Ultrasonography</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> 2 or 3 dimensional image created by the interaction of ultrasonic waves and tissues based on the rates of transmission, reflection and deflection between the source / detector probe
<li> Not widely available and utilized mostly as a research modality (<a href="34292383" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2021;135:2363</a>)
<ul>
<li> Requires additional training in operation and interpretation, which is not widely available outside of radiology training
</ul>
<li> Good at detecting air and fluid collections or areas of largely differing density
<li> May be used to target areas of interest in limited dissection sampling
<li> Significant postmortem artifacts may occur, especially with decompositional changes or heavily refrigerated individuals
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="postmortemangiography467929"><div class="topicheading_title">Postmortem angiography</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Postmortem angiography can be used with Xray, CT or MRI modalities (<a href="17312075" class="bl">AJR Am J Roentgenol 2007;188:832</a>)
<li> Isolated vessel angiography can be used to identify injuries (such as vertebral artery dissections or sharp / projectile injuries in the extremities)
<ul>
<li> Segment of vessel can be isolated using clamps and contrast can be injected into the segment to look for extravasation or luminal irregularity
</ul>
<li> Whole body angiography can be used for better quality imaging 
<ul>
<li> Requires washout of clot and a reperfusion pump
</ul>
<li> If contrast is introduced at too high of pressure, it may result in rupture of blood vessels and artifactual extravasation of contrast
<li> Traditional dissection may still be needed in cases with subtle or no findings when they are suspected
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="identification467930"><div class="topicheading_title">Identification</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li> Postmortem imaging is very frequently used as a means of identification in persons that are not visually identifiable (decomposition, thermal changes, mass disasters, etc.) (<a href="36353311" class="bl">Forensic Sci Res 2022;7:385</a>)
<li> Requires comparison of antemortem images with postmortem images
<ul>
<li> Cannot use a written report for comparison purposes
<li> Having antemortem images available prior to examination can help limit the number of postmortem images needed and may guide positioning of the body to obtain correct orientation
</ul>
<li> Features useful in postmortem identification
<ul>
<li> Unique anatomic findings
<ul>
<li> Frontal sinus pattern (only in skeletally mature individuals; generally &gt; 18 - 20 years of age)
<li> Skeletal abnormalities (i.e., fused ribs, cervical ribs, unfused vertebrae, etc.)
<li> Degenerative changes (i.e., osteophytic lipping, degenerative joint changes, diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis, etc.)
<li> Dystrophic calcifications
</ul>
<li> Remote injuries with healing (i.e., fracture callus)
<li> Medical implants
<ul>
<li> Orthopedic hardware
<li> Implanted medical devices
<li> Surgical clips / staples
</ul>
<li> Dental radiographs (<a href="26015728" class="bl">J Pharm Bioallied Sci 2015;7:S260</a>)
<ul>
<li> Dental implants / fillings
<li> Dental root morphology
<li> Unique bone features
</ul>
</ul>
<li> May also be useful to exclude individuals
<li> Identification should not be based on a single unique feature but instead on an aggregate of multiple distinct features (<a href="26999427" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 2016;37:86</a>)
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="radiologyimages467931"><div class="topicheading_title">Radiology images</div><div class="block_body"><br><span class="f11bi">Contributed by Mark A. Giffen, Jr., D.O.</span><br>

<br>
A person is found obviously deceased in their residence and no resuscitation is attempted. At autopsy, the image above is obtained. What is the most likely cause of the finding in the Xray?
<br><br>
<ol class="liststyle2">
<li> Aortopleural fistula
<li> Blunt trauma
<li> Decomposition
<li> Gunshot wound to the neck
<li> Iatrogenic venous air introduction
</ol>

</div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer1467935"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #1</div><div class="block_body"><b>D.</b> Gunshot wound to the neck. Vascular injuries to the neck (especially venous injuries) can result in traumatic air embolism due to the high concentration of vascular structures in that region. Answer A is incorrect because aortopleural fistula would result in hemothorax, which is not seen in the image and is unlikely to cause significant air embolization. Answer B is incorrect because blunt trauma may result in subcutaneous emphysema, pneumocephalus, thoracic or abdominal air collection but is not a common cause of cardiac air collections. Answer C is incorrect because decomposition can cause false air collections within vasculature but other signs of decomposition are not present in this Xray. Answer E is incorrect because no resuscitative efforts were attempted so iatrogenic embolization could not have occurred.

</div></div><div class="block_section" id="practicequestion2467936"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice question #2</div><div class="block_body">Which of the following can computed tomography allow for which traditional Xray techniques cannot?
<br><br>
<ol class="liststyle2">
<li> Accurate measurement of findings
<li> Angiography
<li> Detection of air and fluid collections
<li> Detection of metallic foreign bodies
<li> Detection of soft tissue injuries
</ol></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer2467937"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #2</div><div class="block_body"><b>A.</b> Accurate measurement of findings. Because traditional Xray techniques are projectional, measurements cannot accurately be determined. Answer B is incorrect because angiography can be performed in either modality. Answers C and E are incorrect because air and fluid collections and soft tissue injuries may be better visualized by computed tomography but can also be seen in Xrays. Answer D is incorrect because metallic foreign bodies can be detected by either technique.
