	</div><div class="table_of_contents"><div class="topicheading_title toc">Table of Contents</div><a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#definitiongeneral498096" class="scrollto">Definition / general</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#essentialfeatures498097" class="scrollto">Essential features</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#matrixandsamplerequirements498102" class="scrollto">Matrix and sample requirements </a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#diagnosticissues498103" class="scrollto">Diagnostic issues</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#furtheravailabletests498104" class="scrollto">Further available tests</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#casereports498105" class="scrollto">Case reports</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#additionalreferences498108" class="scrollto">Additional references</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#practicequestion1498109" class="scrollto">Practice question #1</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#practiceanswer1498110" class="scrollto">Practice answer #1</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#practicequestion2498111" class="scrollto">Practice question #2</a> | <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html#practiceanswer2498112" class="scrollto">Practice answer #2</a></div><div class="table_of_contents" style="word-break: break-all;-webkit-hyphens: auto;-moz-hyphens: auto; hyphens: auto;"><strong>Cite this page:</strong> Tarozzi I, Gitto L. Postmortem chemistry. PathologyOutlines.com website. https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html.  Accessed June 17th, 2025.</div><div class="block_content"><div class="block_section" id="definitiongeneral498096"><div class="topicheading_title">Definition / general</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Postmortem chemistry is a critical ancillary procedure for the forensic pathologist 
<li>Analytes currently being used for evaluation include many biological fluids in addition to blood
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="essentialfeatures498097"><div class="topicheading_title">Essential features</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Biochemical analytes provide useful information in deaths when autopsy findings are nonspecific or no apparent cause of death is identified
<li>Circumstantial data and medical history can give information on whether vitreous chemistry analysis is required or not
<li>Vitreous glucose is the most reliable marker of acute antemortem hyperglycemia
<li>Vitreous potassium concentration of &gt; 15 mmol/L suggests postmortem decomposition or delayed testing and the other analytes should be considered with caution
<li>Acetone, isopropyl alcohol and &beta; hydroxybutyrate are useful markers of ketoacidosis
<li>&beta; hydroxybutyrate has been suggested as a marker of hypothermia
<li>Serum tryptase and immunoglobulin E are useful markers of anaphylaxis
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="matrixandsamplerequirements498102"><div class="topicheading_title">Matrix and sample requirements </div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Vitreous humor
<ul>
<li>Collected by direct gentle needle aspiration from each eye, using a syringe and needle inserted in the outer corner until the tip is placed centrally in the globe (<a href="29460610" class="bl">Arch Med Sadowej Kryminol 2017;67:201</a>)
<li>Less subject to putrefaction than blood, not subject to diffusion of microbes or xenobiotics from the central body cavities (<a href="26793276" class="bl">Forensic Toxicol 2016;34:12</a>)
<li>Recommended quantity: all available (2 - 5 mL in adults, 1 mL in newborns)
<li>Generally collected in a red top tube with no preservatives
<li>Quick rise of the vitreous potassium after death prevents determination of antemortem potassium status
<ul>
<li>Vitreous potassium concentration &gt; 15 mmol/L suggests postmortem decomposition or delayed testing
<li>Remaining analytes should be considered with caution
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Blood
<ul>
<li>Cardiac blood is useful for qualitative / screening analyses
<ul>
<li>Generally collected from the right chambers of the heart or proximal aorta
<li>Collected in a gray top tube (preservative: sodium fluoride) 
<li>Recommended quantity: heart blood 25 mL
</ul>
<li>Peripheral blood is the matrix of choice for quantitative analysis
<ul>
<li>Generally collected from the inferior vena cava or femoral vein (<a href="16042505" class="bl">Toxicol Rev 2005;24:63</a>)
<li>Collected in a gray top tube (preservative: sodium fluoride) 
<li>Recommended quantities: peripheral blood 10 mL from a ligated femoral vein / up to 50 mL if part of the blood is drawn from the inferior vena cava
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Urine
<ul>
<li>Used for qualitative screening
<ul>
<li>Gives information on recent drug use but not on acute toxicity (<a href="21084528" class="bl">Hum Exp Toxicol 2011;30:1165</a>)
</ul>
<li>Collected by a needle aspiration from lower abdominal wall above the pubic symphysis or by urethral catheterization (if external examination only) or directly from the bladder (if internal examination)
<li>Collected in a plastic tube with no preservatives
<li>Recommended quantity: all available up to 50 mL
</ul>
<li>Tissues
<ul>
<li>Mostly liver for biochemical purposes (<a href="15172074" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2004;142:101</a>)
<li>Skeletal muscle can be collected in case of advanced decomposition
<li>Spleen can be used for carbon monoxide analysis when blood is unavailable
<li>Collected in a container with no preservatives
<li>Recommended quantity: 30 - 50 g
</ul>
<li>Hair
<ul>
<li>Many different types of drugs and toxins can be incorporated into hair, which can be used for testing in suspected chronic exposure (<a href="8138221" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 1993;63:19</a>)
<li>Obtained by pulling to include hair roots
<li>Tied tightly together with ligature to maintain orientation
<li>Axillary, pubic, arm or beard hair can be used if head hair is unavailable or unsuitable
<li>Recommended quantity: 50 g
</ul>
<li>Cerebrospinal fluid
<ul>
<li>Qualitative analysis
<li>Can be used for postmortem microbiology analysis (<a href="30927078" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2019;133:1141</a>)
<li>Collected through percutaneous suboccipital / lumbar puncture before the internal examination
<li>Alternatively aspirated from the posterior fossa / lateral ventricles after the brain's removal
<li>Recommended quantity: all available up to 10 mL
<li>Collected in a plain universal tube / container with no preservatives 
</ul>
<li>Bile (<a href="31240045" class="bl">Acad Forensic Pathol 2018;8:324</a>)
<ul>
<li>For qualitative screening and to determine what drugs an individual used or was exposed to prior to death 
<li>Collected by aspiration from gallbladder or directly from common bile duct if cholecystectomy was performed
<li>Always obtained before eviscerating the liver to avoid contamination
<li>Recommended quantity: all available up to 10 mL
</ul>
<li>Synovial fluid (<a href="24781403" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 2014;35:154</a>)
<ul>
<li>Collected from each uninjured joint cavity by lateral puncture of the bursa sac under the patella
<li>Recommended quantity: all available (1 - 2 mL by each knee joint)
<li>Substitute for vitreous humor, if not available 
</ul>
<li>Pericardial fluid
<ul>
<li>Collected through a syringe (no needle) from the pericardial cavity
<li>Recommended quantity: all available (5 - 20 mL)
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="diagnosticissues498103"><div class="topicheading_title">Diagnostic issues</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Disorders of glucose metabolism
<ul>
<li>Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) are acute metabolic complications of diabetes mellitus
<li>Distinguishing between diabetic and alcoholic ketoacidosis can be challenging
<li>Most useful biochemical analytes (<a href="26088843" class="bl">Croat Med J 2015;56:181</a>)
<ul>
<li>Vitreous glucose: most reliable indicator of antemortem hyperglycemia (DKA, HHS)
<li>Blood glycated hemoglobin HbA1c: marker of long term (8 - 12 weeks) antemortem blood glucose control (DKA, HHS)
<li>Blood / vitreous / pericardial fluid &beta; hydroxybutyrate (3HB) and acetone: 3HB is a better postmortem indicator of ketoacidosis (pathologically significant levels > 2,500 &mu;mol/L); elevated acetone levels indicate the need for 3HB measurement (<a href="24334515" class="bl">Alcohol Alcohol 2014;49:271</a>)
<li>Serum / vitreous  / pericardial fluid urea nitrogen, creatinine, urate
<ul>
<li>Vitreous sodium and chloride: increased urea nitrogen, urate, sodium and chloride with normal creatinine indicate volume depletion (DKA, HHS)
</ul>
<li>Vitreous lactate: increased levels possibly due to underlying complications such as sepsis, pneumonia related hypoxia, severe hepatic disorders (<a href="26459058" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2015;129:1225</a>)
<li>Blood / vitreous carbohydrate deficient transferrin (CDT): marker of chronic heavy ethanol consumption
<li>Blood prealbumin and albumin: may indicate undernourishment if low
</ul>
<li>Other analytes
<ul>
<li>Urine glucose: use as a confirmatory test only after analysis of the other markers of hyperglycemia (DKA, HHS)
<li>Blood / vitreous isopropyl alcohol: nonspecific marker of ketoacidosis (DKA, AKA, ALA)
<li>Blood / vitreous fructosamine: marker of 1 - 3 weeks glycemic control but large deviation from living subjects (DKA, HHS) (<a href="10460416" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 1999;112:275</a>)
<li>Vitreous ethyl glucuronide and ethyl sulphate: rarely used to distinguish ethanol ingestion from postmortem formation 
<li>C reactive protein and proinflammatory cytokines: increased levels even without signs of underlying infections (DKA, AKA)
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Dehydration
<ul>
<li>Vitreous sodium, chloride, urea and creatinine are usually increased in cases of hypernatremic dehydration 
<li>Vitreous sodium and chloride are decreased in hyponatremic dehydration
<li>Vitreous urea and creatinine are increased while sodium and chloride remain normal in cases of isonatremic dehydration 
<li>Reference values should be considered with care because they are in a very narrow range
<li>Postmortem diagnosis should rely on toxicological results and autopsy findings (sunken eyes, tinting of skin, poor skin turgor) (<a href="16216704" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2005;155:1</a>)
</ul>
<li>Insulin toxicity related deaths
<ul>
<li>Challenging to prove
<li>Reported among both diabetic and nondiabetic individuals
<li>Scene investigation and a comprehensive medical record review are essential to determine the contributory role of insulin to death (<a href="31239889" class="bl">Acad Forensic Pathol 2016;6:174</a>)
<li>Most useful biochemical analytes (<a href="31239889" class="bl">Acad Forensic Pathol 2016;6:174</a>)
<ul>
<li>Serum insulin: measured on peripheral blood
<li>Serum C peptide: connecting peptide of A and B chains of proinsulin; suppressed levels with elevated insulin levels in exogenous insulin administration
</ul>
<li>Freezing the samples as soon as possible is required to avoid the rapid degradation of insulin at room temperature 
<li>Long hypoglycemic coma allows for metabolism and elimination of insulin before death
<li>Immunohistochemical detection of insulin at the injection site may be helpful
</ul>
<li>Anaphylaxis 
<ul>
<li>Acute immunologic multiorgan (cutaneous, gastrointestinal, respiratory and cardiovascular) response following the exposure to an antigen 
<li>Common stimuli include insects, food, medications and radiocontrast media
<li>Frequently lacks autopsy findings of airway obstruction (laryngeal / epiglottic edema, mucus plugging in the bronchi) due to rapidly developing shock (<a href="27572893" class="bl">Forensic Sci Med Pathol 2017;13:269</a>)
<li>Biochemical testing should be based on circumstances of the death and the individual's medical history
<li>Most useful biochemical analytes
<ul>
<li>Serum &beta; tryptase: neutral protease released following mast cell IgE induced degranulation; sampling from femoral vein to avoid confounding effect of resuscitation induced tryptase release from the heart (<a href="32717658" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2020;314:110415</a>)
<li>Serum immunoglobulin E (IgE) levels: markers of atopic disposition (total IgE) and prior sensitization (specific IgE; only if the offending antigen is known / suspected)
</ul>
<li>Other analytes
<ul>
<li>Chymase: mast cell derived neutral protease; positive correlation with tryptase levels
<li>Histamine: amine stored in the secretory granules of mast cells; rapid degradation, unreliable postmortem marker
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Hypothermia
<ul>
<li>Core body temperature at / below 35 &deg;C (95 &deg;F)
<li>No pathognomonic signs at autopsy (diagnosis of exclusion)
<li>Most useful analytes
<ul>
<li>Blood / vitreous / pericardial fluid / urine / cerebrospinal fluid &beta; hydroxybutyrate (3HB) and acetone: there is an inverse statistically significant relationship between ketone body levels in various fluids and ethanol blood levels (<a href="24557588" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2014;128:607</a>)
<li>Blood / vitreous / pericardial fluid / urine / cerebrospinal fluid isopropyl alcohol: nonspecific marker of ketoacidosis
<li>Serum / urine cortisol, urine catecholamines: increased levels may support the diagnosis but normal levels do not exclude it (<a href="29715177" class="bl">Clin Chem Lab Med 2018;56:1819</a>)
</ul>
<li>Ethanol is the most commonly detected drug and can accelerate body cooling, preventing morphologic and biochemical changes from occurring
</ul>
<li>Drowning
<ul>
<li>Death caused by submersion in a liquid
<li>No pathognomonic autopsy findings (diagnosis of exclusion)
<li>Circumstances of the event are critical to determine cause and manner of death
<li>Suggested analytes
<ul>
<li>Vitreous sodium and serum chloride: hemodilution (low sodium) with left heart blood chloride < right heart blood chloride in fresh water drowning; hemoconcentration (high sodium) with left heart blood chloride > right heart blood chloride in salt water drowning
<li>Serum and pericardial fluid calcium and magnesium: increased levels in both peripheral and central blood reported in saltwater drowning 
<li>Serum strontium: increased levels in central blood from left ventricle reported in saltwater and freshwater drownings (<a href="21873008" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2012;214:159</a>)
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Sepsis
<ul>
<li>Systemic inflammatory response syndrome triggered by an infection, leading to acute organ dysfunction (severe sepsis) and perfusion abnormalities due to either refractory hypotension or hyperlactatemia (septic shock)
<li>Nonspecific and often controversial autopsy findings
<li>Most useful analytes (<a href="24778096" class="bl">Croat Med J 2014;55:103</a>)
<ul>
<li>Serum procalcitonin (PCT)
<ul>
<li>Long half life (25 - 30 hours)
<li>Very stable even at room temperature
<li>Increased levels in severe systemic bacterial, parasitic or fungal infections (<a href="16787725" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2007;165:155</a>) 
</ul>
<li>Serum / liver C reactive protein and serum proinflammatory cytokines: increased levels in acute phase response to inflammatory tissue injury
</ul>
<li>Other analytes
<ul>
<li>Serum interleukin 2 receptor (sIL2R): marker of lymphocyte activation
<li>Serum lipopolysaccharide binding protein: opsonin
<li>Serum / urine neopterin: produced upon IFN&gamma; stimulation; elevated concentrations in viral, bacterial, protozoic, parasitic or fungal systemic infections
</ul>
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="furtheravailabletests498104"><div class="topicheading_title">Further available tests</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Renal function
<ul>
<li>Serum / vitreous / pericardial fluid urea nitrogen, urate and creatinine, vitreous sodium and chloride are reliable markers 
<li>Useful in cases of neglect (increased urea nitrogen, urate, sodium and chloride with normal creatinine), salt poisoning (rapid onset of hypernatremia and hyperchloremia) and water intoxication (marked hyponatremia and hypochloremia) (<a href="14514793" class="bl">J Clin Pathol 2003;56:803</a>)
</ul>
<li>Liver function
<ul>
<li>Total cholesterol, low serum proteins with inversion of albumin:globulin ratio and high serum bilirubin are reliable indicators of advanced liver disease (<a href="18511006" class="bl">J Forensic Leg Med 2008;15:312</a>)
<li>Postmortem serum transaminase levels are variable, unpredictable and independent of antemortem values
</ul>
<li>Cardiac function
<ul>
<li>Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) and brain natriuretic peptide (BNP): increased levels in pericardial fluid reported in chronic congestive heart disease cases (<a href="16741745" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2007;121:28</a>)
<li>Troponin I (cTnI), troponin T (cTnT) and creatine kinase (CKMB): in cases of myocardial ischemia, cTnI and cTnT increase depends on the severity of myocardial damage at the time of death
</ul>
<li>Hormones
<ul>
<li>Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH): low levels in cerebrospinal fluid observed in cases of prolonged hypothermia (<a href="18554831" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2008;179:147</a>, <a href="11444669" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 2001;22:78</a>)
<li>Chromogranin A: low serum and high cerebrospinal fluid levels described in cases of fatal hypothermia (<a href="19760428" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2011;125:11</a>)
<li>Thyroglobulin (TG), triiodothyronine (T3): increased central blood levels reported in cases of incomplete suspension and manual strangulation (<a href="9493331" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 1997;90:165</a>)
<li>Chorionic gonadotropin (HCG): positive in pregnant women (serum, vitreous, bile) and postmortem levels correspond to antemortem levels; may also be produced by choriocarcinoma or related tumors (<a href="20345805" class="bl">J Forensic Sci 2010;55:792</a>)
<li>Erythropoietin (EPO): survival time dependent postmortem serum elevation within 6 hours in deaths due to acute massive hemorrhage (<a href="18682967" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2008;122:481</a>)
<li>Serotonin (5-HT): some authors observed elevated levels in deaths due to sedative / hypnotic drug intoxication and hyperthermia (<a href="15172080" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2004;142:211</a>)
</ul>
<li>Miscellaneous markers
<ul>
<li>S100B: possible marker of astrocyte injury in cases of hypoxic cerebral damage due to mechanical asphyxia (<a href="16337822" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2006;8:71</a>)
<li>Myoglobin: elevated levels reported in cases of massive skeletal muscle damage (such as heat stroke, fatal burns, convulsive disorders, muscle hyperactivity) (<a href="11074173" class="bl">Forensic Sci Int 2001;115:183</a>)
<li>Sudden unexpected death in infancy and childhood: skin sample for fibroblast culture and biochemical analysis on urine (if present), blood and bile spots on Guthrie card for inherited metabolic disorders; muscle biopsy if history is suggestive of mitochondrial disorder (<a href="19282226" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2009;11:S36</a>)
<li>Sudden cardiac death: serum, vitreous humor, urine, pericardial / cerebrospinal fluid samples should be collected for biochemistry analyses; the diagnostic value of the combination of high sensitivity cardiac troponin t (hs-cTnT), N-terminal pro b type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP) and pentraxin 3 in postmortem serum has been recently investigated (<a href="36738552" class="bl">Leg Med (Tokyo) 2023;61:102212</a>)
<li>Genetic epileptic disorders: inherited metabolic disorders are infrequent but often treatable causes of epilepsy; the initial metabolic screen includes blood glucose, electrolytes, lactate, ammonia, urinary ketones, plasma amino acids, acylcarnitines, urinary organic acids and plasma sphingolipids (<a href="28671587" class="bl">Int J Mol Sci 2017;18:1384</a>)
<li>Postmortem interval estimation: numerous analytes have been proposed but none are currently validated
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="casereports498105"><div class="topicheading_title">Case reports</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>31 year old man with a history of type 1 diabetes mellitus and diabetic ketoacidosis (<a href="30856551" class="bl">J Forensic Leg Med 2019;63:34</a>) 
<li>38 year old man and two 58 year old men with anaphylaxis due to bee stings (<a href="26759134" class="bl">Forensic Sci Med Pathol 2016;12:81</a>)
<li>54 year old man with psychiatric history and fatal water intoxication (<a href="28503702" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2018;132:173</a>)
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="additionalreferences498108"><div class="topicheading_title">Additional references</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li><a href="8328447" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 1993;14:91</a>, <a href="21947676" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2012;126:187</a>, <a href="21984165" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2012;126:199</a>, <a href="27131037" class="bl">J Forensic Leg Med 2016;41:49</a>, <a href="30483610" class="bl">Forensic Sci Res 2017;1:42</a>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practicequestion1498109"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice question #1</div><div class="block_body">A 40 year old man with a history of obesity, chronic alcoholism and diabetes mellitus type 1 was found unresponsive during a well being check. He was last known to be alive the night before and he was witnessed drinking large amounts of alcohol. Autopsy examination showed liver steatosis but no other significant pathologic changes. Toxicology and blood and vitreous chemistry analyses showed the following results
<br><br>
<ul>
<li>Inferior vena cava blood
<ul>
<li>&beta; hydroxybutyric acid: > 1,200 mcg/mL (normal: < 50 mcg/mL)
<li>Ethanol: 78 mg/dL (reporting limit: 10 mg/dL)
<li>Isopropanol: 6.6 mg/dL (reporting limit: 5.0 mg/dL)  
<li>Acetone: 42 mg/dL (normal: < 0.3 mg/dL)
<li>Caffeine: presumptive pos (reporting limit: 0.20 mcg/mL)
</ul>
<li>Vitreous
<ul>
<li>Creatinine: 0.875 mg/dL (normal: 0.6 - 1.3 mg/dL)
<li>Sodium: 143 mmol/L (normal: < 15 mmol/L)
<li>Potassium: 14.3 mmol/L (normal: < 15 mmol/L)
<li>Chloride: 118 mmol/L (normal: 105 - 135 mmol/L)
<li>Glucose: 327 mg/dL (normal: < 200 mg/dL)
<li>Urea nitrogen: 27 mg/dL (normal: 8 - 20 mg/dL)
</ul>
</ul>
<br>
Which of the following causes of death is best certified?
<br><br>
<ol class="liststyle2">
<li>Acute ethanol toxicity
<li>Alcoholic ketoacidosis
<li>Chronic alcoholism 
<li>Diabetic ketoacidosis
<li>Multifactorial ketoacidosis
</ol></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer1498110"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #1</div><div class="block_body"><b>E</b>. Multifactorial ketoacidosis. The deceased had a history of chronic alcoholism and diabetes mellitus. Postmortem chemistry shows a high concentration of ketones and glucose in the collected samples. Since alcohol, diabetes and starvation can result in ketoacidosis, the cause of death should be certified as multifactorial ketoacidosis (ketoacidosis alone is also an acceptable option).
<br><br>
Answer A is incorrect because the concentration of alcohol in peripheral blood is not enough to cause acute toxicity, even if there is a history of recent consumption of large quantities of ethanol. Answers B and D are incorrect because there is no reliable way to distinguish between alcoholic and diabetic ketoacidosis in this case since both conditions can lead to it. Answer C is incorrect because even if there is liver steatosis, suggesting chronic ethanol use, the high levels of ketones are consistent with ketoacidosis, making that the more immediate cause of death.
<br><br>

<br><br>
Reference: <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html">Postmortem chemistry</a></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practicequestion2498111"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice question #2</div><div class="block_body">The body of a 64 year old woman is discovered supine on the floor in her secured residence after an unknown interval. There is no available medical or social history. External and internal examination reveal a well developed and well nourished woman showing no evidence of trauma. Brown colored vitreous fluid and liver samples are collected and submitted for analysis. Blood samples could not be collected. Which of the following should be considered most important while interpreting the results of the available samples?
<br><br>
<ol class="liststyle2">
<li>Absence or presence of glucose in vitreous fluid is of no significance
<li>Color of the vitreous fluid is suspicious for trauma to the eye; a more detailed inspection is required
<li>Liver tissue is the best specimen for quantitative analyses 
<li>Potassium concentration in vitreous fluid
<li>Sodium concentration in vitreous fluid
</ol></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer2498112"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #2</div><div class="block_body"><b>D</b>. Potassium concentration in vitreous fluid. Checking the concentration of vitreous potassium is the first step to determine whether the concentration of other analytes is relevant or should be considered with caution. A vitreous potassium level above 15 mmol/L suggests that decomposition changes may contribute to the observed result. In the provided history, the liver was collected and the vitreous fluid was brownish in color, further suggesting that the body was at least in an early stage of decomposition.
<br><br>
Answer A is incorrect because glucose concentration is a useful marker of diabetes or a hyperglycemic state. Answer C is incorrect because the best and most reliable postmortem specimen for quantitative analysis is peripheral blood. Answer B is incorrect because while in a fresh body, a red tinged vitreous fluid may suggest local trauma or sampling artifact, in a decomposed body, a darker color in the absence of clear evidence of injury should be considered a postmortem artifact. Answer E is incorrect because the sodium concentration in vitreous fluid is a useful marker of dehydration; however, its concentration should be considered after determining the potassium concentration.
