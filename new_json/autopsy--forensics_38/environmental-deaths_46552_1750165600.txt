Environmental deaths. PathologyOutlines.com website. https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicsenvironmentaldeaths.html.  Accessed June 17th, 2025.</div><div class="block_content"><div class="block_section" id="definitiongeneral488722"><div class="topicheading_title">Definition / general</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Death due to exogenous environmental factors, exposure or injury
<li>May be due to natural phenomena, unintentional human exposure or intentional human intervention
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="essentialfeatures488723"><div class="topicheading_title">Essential features</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Burn injury severity can vary significantly depending on a number of intrinsic and external factors and may result in immediate or delayed death
<li>Careful examination is required to determine antemortem thermal injuries from postmortem thermal artifacts
<li>Electrical injuries may present with minimal external signs and require careful scene investigation
<li>Hypothermia and hyperthermia have no specific postmortem findings and diagnosis may be dependent on investigation, supportive findings and lack of another cause of death
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="firerelateddeaths488726"><div class="topicheading_title">Fire related deaths</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Includes death due to burns, inhalation of heated gases / products of combustion or subsequent complications that may arise from either
<li>Classification of burns (<a href="32054846" class="bl">Nat Rev Dis Primers 2020;6:11</a>)
<ul>
<li>First degree: limited to epidermis; erythema, edema and pain; no scarring
<li>Second degree: involves the epidermis and dermis (superficial or deep); erythema, edema and skin blistering with pain, usually without scarring
<li>Third degree: involves the full epidermis and dermis; dry, yellow or white and painless; scarring
<li>Fourth degree: involves full thickness epidermis, dermis and underlying tissues (adipose, muscle or bone); black and charred appearance
<li>Note: the clinical distinction between deep second degree and third degree burns often involves determination of the sensation of pain, which cannot be assessed at autopsy
</ul>
<li>Documentation of burns
<ul>
<li>Most commonly used method of burn estimation in the United States is the (Wallace) Rule of Nines to determine the total body surface area (TBSA) involved (<a href="30020659" class="bl">StatPearls: Rule of Nines [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
<ul>
<li>Rule of Nines distributes each major body area involved as 9% of the total surface area and 1% if there is groin / perineal involvement
<li>Rule of Nines only applies to adult patients, with modifications for infant and pediatric patients
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Factors impacting burn severity include
<ul>
<li>Heat intensity, duration of exposure to source, presence or absence of intermediate objects (e.g., clothing, furniture), type of heat source, other concomitant injuries, intoxication and comorbid health conditions
</ul>
<li>Types of burns (<a href="15191982" class="bl">BMJ 2004;328:1427</a>)
<ul>
<li>Scald burn: caused by contact with hot liquid or gas
<ul>
<li>Common in children and the elderly
<li>Pattern should match with mechanism of injury; otherwise, consider the possibility of inflicted trauma
<li>May see typical pour pattern with spilled / poured hot liquids with worst burn at site of initial, most concentrated contact
<li>Also assess for respiratory injury with involvement of the face and head
</ul>
<li>Flame burn: caused by contact with flame (such as in residential house fires)
<ul>
<li>Flash burns are a subset of flame burns that occur due to sudden ignition of a flammable source which results in uniform burns over all exposed areas
</ul>
<li>Contact burn: caused by direct contact between a hot object and the skin
<ul>
<li>May result in a patterned injury mimicking contacted surface
<ul>
<li>Pattern may become distorted as healing occurs
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Radiant / radiation burn: caused by exposure to transmitted heat waves, electromagnetic waves or radioactive substances
<ul>
<li>Contact does not need to occur between source and skin
<li>Examples: prolonged exposure to extreme heat elements (e.g., industrial furnaces), microwave burns, ultraviolet rays (e.g., sunburn)
<li>If exposure to radioactive materials is suspected, extreme isolation precautions should be taken as first responders and secondary personnel may also risk exposure (<a href="https://www.cdc.gov/nceh/radiation/emergencies/medicalexaminers.htm" class="bl">CDC: Radiation Emergencies - Information for Medical Examiners, Coroners, and Funeral Home Personnel [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
</ul>
<li>Chemical burn: not a type of thermal injury but a common mimic; caused by exposure to strong acidic or alkali chemicals
<ul>
<li>Acidic burns tend to be partial thickness and less severe
<li>Alkali burns tend to be deeper and cause full thickness injury
<li>Injury may develop more slowly than thermal burns
</ul>
<li>Common findings at autopsy which may mimic burns
<ul>
<li>Decompositional skin slippage and postmortem bullae
<li>Prolonged purge fluid exposure to skin
<li>Antemortem conditions including drug reactions, autoimmune disease and infection (<a href="21990994" class="bl">Ann Burns Fire Disasters 2005;18:130</a>)
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Postmortem changes and artifacts in burned / charred bodies (<a href="28980554" class="bl">Sud Med Ekspert 2017;60:44</a>)
<ul>
<li>Pugilistic posture
<li>Thermal skin splitting
<li>Thermal fracture of long bones and the skull / amputation
<li>Thermal intrusion of body cavities
<li>Thermal epidural hematoma
<li>Pseudocerebral edema
</ul>
<li>Mechanism of death (<a href="32054846" class="bl">Nat Rev Dis Primers 2020;6:11</a>)
<ul>
<li>Immediate mechanisms
<ul>
<li>Neurogenic or hypovolemic shock
<li>Toxic gas exposure (<a href="12184505" class="bl">Crit Rev Toxicol 2002;32:259</a>)
<ul>
<li>Carbon monoxide (CO): levels may reach 30 - 60% or greater
<ul>
<li>Consider testing for CO if the victim is found in an enclosed space such as a vehicle or a garage
<li>May be lower with coexisting injuries or comorbid disease
<li>Often see soot or evidence of thermal injuries in airways
<li>Level of ~10% can be detected in smokers
<li>If a death in a residential building is attributed to CO toxicity, alert the authorities to prevent other potential fatalities

</ul>
<li>Hydrogen cyanide compounds
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Delayed mechanisms
<ul>
<li>Hypovolemic shock with renal failure
<li>Hypermetabolic state with multisystem organ failure
<li>Secondary respiratory disorders (e.g., acute respiratory distress syndrome)
<li>Infection (e.g., pneumonia, cutaneous or systemic)
<li>Secondary coagulation disorders (e.g., pulmonary embolism, disseminated intravascular coagulation)
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Scene investigation
<ul>
<li>Often limited due to unsafe environmental factors
<li>Conducted with law enforcement and fire personnel to determine source of fire
<li>Collection of specialized evidence (e.g., clothing for accelerants or volatiles) may be warranted (<a href="17456085" class="bl">J Forensic Sci 2007;52:579</a>)
</ul>
<li>Postmortem examination
<ul>
<li>Postmortem radiology is critical to identify potential retained foreign bodies (bullets, fragments, blades, etc.)
<li>Detailed external examination to detect injuries that are not consistent with charring / burns
<li>Recognize heat related artifact (see above)
</ul>
<li>Manner of death (<a href="https://name.memberclicks.net/assets/docs/MANNEROFDEATH.pdf" class="bl">NAME: A Guide For Manner of Death Classification [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
<ul>
<li>Manner mainly relies on fire investigation and postmortem findings
<li>Would be determined by the underlying source of the fire / thermal injury
<li>Most fire related deaths are accidental
<ul>
<li>Even if an ignition source cannot ultimately be determined, if one can reasonably exclude intentionally set fire, the manner of death may still be classified as accident rather than undetermined
<li>However, if the circumstances of the event are unclear, an undetermined manner of death should be used
</ul>
<li>Death due to an intentionally set fire (arson) would be classified as homicide
<li>Death due to a person setting themselves or their environment on fire (self immolation) would be classified as suicide
<li>Similar to other manner of death classifications, if the injury resulted in or significantly contributed to the death, the manner of death should reflect the underlying cause of the injury
<ul>
<li>Some burn victims may live for days, weeks or months before succumbing to delayed complications of the thermal / inhalational injury
</ul>
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="electricalinjuriesdeath488727"><div class="topicheading_title">Electrical injuries / death</div><div class="block_body"><ul>
<li>Immediate or delayed death due to exposure to an electrical current
<li>Transmission of energy dictated by Ohm's law (<a href="28404561" class="bl">BMJ 2017;357:j1418</a>)
<ul>
<li>I = V/R
<li>Current (I): measure of flow of energy through an object / circuit
<li>Voltage (V): unit charge of the source (dependent on the electrical source)
<ul>
<li>Common household voltage in the United States ranges from 120 to 240 volts
<li>Lightning voltage can be into the tens of millions of volts or higher
</ul>
<li>Resistance (R): resistance of the object / circuit
<ul>
<li>Resistance can be increased (less transmission) by wearing protective equipment
<li>Resistance can be decreased (more transmission) with increased surface area of contact or exposure to wet skin / clothing
</ul>
</ul>
<li>2 types of electrical current may be encountered, which may cause distinctly different findings (<a href="28846317" class="bl">StatPearls: Electrical Injuries [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>, <a href="36251236" class="bl">Forensic Sci Med Pathol 2023;19:91</a>)
<ul>
<li>Alternating current (AC)
<ul>
<li>Most common form of electricity in standard applications (household products, most industrial machinery and power lines)
<li>Low voltage electrocution (< 1000 volts)
<ul>
<li>Most commonly used home electrical devices
<li>May have little to no external injury (requires careful external examination and scene investigation)
<li>Gross findings: may leave burns at the entry and exit site
</ul>
<li>High voltage electrocution (> 1000 volts)
<ul>
<li>Some industrial equipment and power lines are common sources
<li>May have enough energy to arc (jump) from the source to the victim
<li>Gross findings: often significant burn / charring at sites of entry and exit
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Direct current (DC)
<ul>
<li>More rarely encountered (lightning is most common source)
<li>May have little to no external signs of injury or significant burns / charring
<li>Ferning pattern of Lichtenberg figures may be seen on the skin surface
<li>Force generated during a lightning strike may throw the victim a significant distance and cause damage to clothing or nearby objects
<ul>
<li>In the correct circumstances, the scene may appear akin to a motor vehicle striking a pedestrian
<li>May also cause secondary blunt injuries if the victim strikes other objects
<li>Lightning strikes can and do occur at the border of storms or during storm development and no rain or other evidence of a storm may be present during investigation
</ul>
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Histologic findings
<ul>
<li>Nuclear streaming / palisading within the epidermis
<li>Separation of the layers of the epidermis or between the epidermis and dermis
</ul>
<li>Mechanism of death
<ul>
<li>Immediate (can be seen in all types of electrical injury): cardiac arrhythmias
<li>Delayed and indirect (generally high voltage injuries)
<ul>
<li>End organ damage (along path of current)
<li>Secondary blunt injuries
<li>Complications of external and internal burn injury
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Scene investigation (<a href="6617121" class="bl">Clin Lab Med 1983;3:343</a>)
<ul>
<li>May be the key to determining electrocution as the cause of death
<li>Disconnect concerning products / power sources
<ul>
<li>It may be necessary to have a certified electrical expert inspect the suspected product / connections 
</ul>
<li>Careful examination of clothing and other objects in the vicinity may help detect subtle electrical entry / exit sites at autopsy
</ul>
<li>Manner of death (<a href="https://name.memberclicks.net/assets/docs/MANNEROFDEATH.pdf" class="bl">NAME: A Guide For Manner of Death Classification [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
<ul>
<li>Manner of death is related to the underlying source of the electrical injury
<li>Manner of death in the vast majority of cases is an accident
<ul>
<li>Work related deaths must be reported so that workers' death benefits can provide financial support for their family
</ul>
<li>Very rarely do electrical injuries occur in homicides or suicides (scene investigation will help identify these cases)
</ul>
</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="temperaturerelateddeaths488728"><div class="topicheading_title">Temperature related deaths</div><div class="block_body"><b>Hypothermia</b>
<br>
<ul>
<li>Defined as a core body temperature < 95&deg;F (35&deg;C) (<a href="23150960" class="bl">N Engl J Med 2012;367:1930</a>)
<li>Can be defined as primary or secondary hypothermia
<ul>
<li>Primary: due to environmental cold exposure overwhelming heat production
<li>Secondary: decreased body temperature as a result of another medical condition (e.g., infection, cardiovascular disease) or medication
</ul>
<li>Increased risk of hypothermia in the very young, very old, thin individuals, persons with injuries and those that are intoxicated (<a href="35010760" class="bl">Int J Environ Res Public Health 2022;19:501</a>)
<li>Autopsy examination (<a href="29715177" class="bl">Clin Chem Lab Med 2018;56:1819</a>)
<ul>
<li>Hypothermia is a diagnosis of exclusion without any specific signs at postmortem examination; must exclude other competing or contributing causes of death
<ul>
<li>Postmortem body temperature is not specific nor reliable; core body temperature during hospital presentation is a much better indicator
</ul>
<li>Inner knee sign (synovial membrane hemorrhage and bloody discoloration of synovial fluid) (<a href="22349758" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2012;126:415</a>)
<li>Cherry red lividity: increased oxyhemoglobin 
<ul>
<li>Exclude potential carbon monoxide poisoning exposure
</ul>
<li>Wischnewski spots: multiple small hemorrhages throughout the gastric mucosa
<ul>
<li>May be seen in other types of deaths (e.g., diabetic ketoacidosis)
</ul>
<li>Microscopic findings
<ul>
<li>Cytoplasmic vacuolization of renal tubule epithelium (Armanni-Ebstein lesions), pancreatic cells, hepatocytes, cardiac myocytes, adrenal glands and pituitary (anterior)
<li>May see focal myocardial necrosis
</ul>
<li>Biomarkers
<ul>
<li>Several biomarkers have been suggested for diagnosis (including catecholamines, cortisol and ketone bodies) but are neither sensitive nor specific
<li>&beta;-hydroxybutyrate, acetone and isopropyl alcohol should be considered (<a href="24557588" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2014;128:607</a>) 	
<ul>
<li>Decomposition changes are not associated with &beta;-hydroxybutyrate production
</ul>
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Mechanism of death
<ul>
<li>Hypermetabolic state resulting from dysregulated autonomic system and multisystem organ failure; may manifest with cardiac dysrhythmias and labile blood pressure
</ul>
<li>Scene investigation
<ul>
<li>Paradoxical undressing: dysregulated hypothalamic system results in a feeling of being overheated; results in disorganized undressing in a delirious state
<li>Hide and die or terminal burrowing: in an attempt to stay warm, individuals may attempt to burrow or crawl into enclosed spaces; may result in minor external trauma
</ul>
<li>Manner of death (<a href="https://name.memberclicks.net/assets/docs/MANNEROFDEATH.pdf" class="bl">NAME: A Guide For Manner of Death Classification [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
<ul>
<li>Primary hypothermia: dependent on factors resulting in terminal cold exposure
<ul>
<li>Generally accidental; rarely may be homicide, such as in the case of a child locked outside and unable to access shelter
</ul>
<li>Secondary hypothermia: related to the underlying condition causing hypothermia and therefore generally natural
<li>If a natural disease causes death in a cold environment, based on the circumstances of the event, the contribution of cold exposure should be considered, making the manner of death accident
</ul>
</ul>
<br>
<b>Hyperthermia</b>
<ul>
<li>Defined as core body temperature > 104&deg;F (40&deg;C) (<a href="9095294" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 1997;18:11</a>)
<li>Can be defined as primary hyperthermia or secondary hyperthermia (<a href="24176481" class="bl">Emerg Med Clin North Am 2013;31:1097</a>)
<ul>
<li>Primary: environmental hyperthermia with or without physical exertion
<li>Secondary
<ul>
<li>Drug associated (anticholinergic drugs, stimulants, serotonin syndrome, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, malignant hyperthermia [inhaled anesthetics])
<li>Infectious
<li>Endocrine abnormalities (thyroid and adrenal hyperexcretion)
<li>Neurologic (status epileptics and cerebral hemorrhage)
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Heat related illnesses occur on a spectrum
<ul>
<li>Heat edema / heat cramps: dependent edema or leg cramping (generally not associated with increased core body temperature); not lethal
<li>Heat exhaustion: elevated body temperature (typically), tachypnea, weakness, nausea / vomiting, diaphoresis; generally not independently lethal (may be contributory with comorbid conditions)
<li>Heat stroke: elevated body temperature, central nervous system dysfunction and multisystem organ failure
</ul>
<li>Increased risk in those with impaired thermoregulatory capacity including the very young, very old, people with medical comorbidities and intoxicated individuals
<li>Autopsy examination (<a href="22669324" class="bl">Int J Legal Med 2013;127:93</a>)
<ul>
<li>Hyperthermia may be a diagnosis of exclusion; need to exclude other competing or contributing causes of death
<ul>
<li>Postmortem body temperature is not specific; core body temperature during hospital presentation is a much better indicator
</ul>
<li>Findings are generally nonspecific and partly dependent on survival interval
<li>Dysregulated coagulation may result in disseminated intravascular coagulation with patchy or florid hemorrhages and small vessel thrombosis / ischemia
<li>Microscopic findings
<ul>
<li>Centrilobular necrosis and midzonal vacuolar changes of the liver
<li>Neuronal degeneration
<li>Acute pancreatitis
<li>Evidence of rhabdomyolysis and cardiac myocyte necrosis
<li>Pulmonary fat embolization
</ul>
<li>Biomarkers
<ul>
<li>Hyperthermia typically occurs in conjunction with dehydration and electrolyte disturbances and therefore vitreous testing is often helpful (see <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicschemistry.html">Vitreous analysis [postmortem chemistry]</a>)
<li>Other biomarkers have been suggested (including catecholamines, pituitary hormones, cardiovascular markers, serotonin, tryptase, myoglobin and procalcitonin) but are neither specific nor sensitive
</ul>
</ul>
<li>Mechanism of death
<ul>
<li>Hypermetabolic state resulting from dysregulated autonomic system and disseminated intravascular coagulation / coagulopathy
</ul>
<li>Scene investigation
<ul>
<li>No specific findings; may have evidence of undressing or abnormal / delirious behavior
<li>Interviews with witnesses may help delineate timeline and behavior
<li>Medication history may be important
</ul>
<li>Manner of death (<a href="https://name.memberclicks.net/assets/docs/MANNEROFDEATH.pdf" class="bl">NAME: A Guide For Manner of Death Classification [Accessed 16 February 2024]</a>)
<ul>
<li>Primary hyperthermia: dependent on factors resulting in terminal heat exposure and physical exertion
<ul>
<li>Generally considered accidental
<li>Forced exertion may be classified as homicide
<li>Pediatric deaths in hot environments may have a wide range of classifications depending on circumstances (<a href="37728903" class="bl">Am J Forensic Med Pathol 2023;44:251</a>)
</ul>
<li>Secondary hyperthermia: related to the underlying condition causing hyperthermia and therefore usually natural
</ul>
</ul>

</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="clinicalimages488729"><div class="topicheading_title">Clinical images</div><div class="block_body"><br><span class="f11bi">Contributed by Mark A. Giffen, Jr., D.O., Jerri L. McLemore, M.D. and Patrick E. Lantz, M.D.</span><br>

</ul></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practicequestion1488734"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice question #1</div><div class="block_body"><div class="img1">
<a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/forensicsenvironmentaldeathsGiffen9.jpg" 
data-lightbox="forensicsenvironmentaldeathsbrq1" data-title="Contributed by Patrick E. Lantz, M.D.">
<img width="96" height="96" alt="" 
src="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/forensicsenvironmentaldeathsGiffen9.jpg" 
onerror="this.src='https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/thumb/NoImage.jpg';"></a>
<p></div>
<br>
A bystander noticed a damaged bicycle sitting on the side of a rural highway; the bicycle belonged to a 63 year old neighbor who often rides in the area for exercise. The bystander searched the area and found the neighbor collapsed ~15 feet from the roadway without apparent external injuries. The ground was dry and there had been no recent rain in the area. During investigation, the decedent's hat was found near the bicycle, as seen in the image above. What is the most likely cause of the decedent's death?

<br><br>

<ol class="liststyle2">
<li>Arrhythmia due to lightning strike
<li>Blunt force injuries from a hit and run motor vehicle collision
<li>Carbon monoxide inhalation
<li>Gunshot wound to the head
<li>Myocardial infarction due to coronary artery disease

</ol></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer1488735"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #1</div><div class="block_body"><b>A.</b> Arrhythmia due to lightning strike. The hat and bicycle demonstrate damage from the forces generated from a lightning strike despite there being no injuries to the decedent. It is important to note that lightning strikes can occur at the border of storms and no evidence may be present by the time of discovery. <br><br>

Answer B is incorrect because of the lack of external evidence of trauma and no mention of vehicle parts or other signs of collision near the bicycle. Autopsy would be helpful to exclude occult injuries from a motor vehicle strike. Answer C is incorrect because carbon monoxide inhalation is extremely unlikely in an outside environment in the absence of an obvious source. Answer D is incorrect because there is no external trauma to the body and no blood or other evidence of a gunshot on the hat. Answer E is incorrect because a myocardial infarction due to coronary artery disease would not explain the damage to the bicycle or the hat.


<br><br>
<a href="#" class="contact_form_trigger">Comment Here</a>

<br><br>
Reference: <a href="https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/forensicsenvironmentaldeaths.html">Environmental deaths</a></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practicequestion2488736"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice question #2</div><div class="block_body">After the fire department extinguishes a fire at a home, a charred body is discovered in the bedroom area. The home belonged to a 75 year old woman who lived alone and was known to smoke in bed. At the autopsy examination, it is noted that the extremities are largely absent with charred exposure of the proximal humeri and femurs and thermal intrusion of the body cavities. No hemorrhage is present in the remaining soft tissues. What is the most likely cause of the amputations and torso changes?

<br><br>

<ol class="liststyle2">
<li>Antemortem injuries
<li>Postmortem amputation due to body entrapment for extrication
<li>Postmortem dismemberment in attempt to hide homicide
<li>Postmortem thermal amputation and tissue loss
<li>Remote antemortem amputation

</ol></div></div><div class="block_section" id="practiceanswer2488737"><div class="topicheading_title">Practice answer #2</div><div class="block_body"><b>D.</b> Postmortem thermal amputation and tissue loss. In a residential house fire, postmortem tissue loss in very common with the hallmarks being loss of the distal and smaller exposed areas progressing proximally to thicker, more robust areas. Often there is a small amount of residual charred soft tissue attached to a calcined portion of exposed long bone. The adjacent soft tissues will not exhibit soft tissue hemorrhage, which would be indicative of antemortem injury.
<br><br>
Answer A is incorrect because of the absence of hemorrhage or other signs of vital response to injury, which are often seen if the body is not largely consumed by thermal changes. Answer B is incorrect because postmortem amputation for extrication purposes would result in clean margins of tissue without thermal changes.  Answer C is incorrect because postmortem dismemberment with secondary burning would lead to atypical patterns of thermal changes and likely other evidence of trauma from the homicidal violence. Answer E is incorrect because antemortem amputation is unlikely to impact all 4 limbs and the margins would be clean and well healed. Antemortem amputation may help to explain the absence of a single or more limbs without significant thermal changes in some circumstances.
